Even your friends who bought a house in 2024 have probably never heard of this, so let me explain what a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement actually is, what it means for you, what to look for before signing, and why it exists to protect you.

## What Is a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement?

A Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement is a written contract between you and your real estate agent. It spells out three things clearly:

- What services your agent will provide
- How long your agreement lasts
- How your agent will be compensated, and by whom

That's it. The agreement exists to make the relationship transparent before any money changes hands or any homes get toured. Before this requirement existed, buyers could work with agents for months without ever having a formal conversation about compensation or expectations. The new law changed that.

[California AB 2992](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billCompareClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240AB2992&showamends=false), which took effect January 1, 2025, requires a signed Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement before your agent can show you any property. This is not optional and it is not something specific to one brokerage. Your agent isn't being a stickler; she's just trying to stay out of real estate jail.

## What Is Actually In the Agreement?

Before you sign anything, here is what to look for:

### How Long Does a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement Last?

Some are for a single showing, while others last for months. The maximum length of a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement is 90 days (unless the buyer is a corporation, LLC, or partnership), and can be extended. Personally, I'm all about the 90-day agreement because it gives you and your agent more time and flexibility to strategize together. Make sure you understand the time commitment before you sign.

### What Services Do Real Estate Agents Provide?

Your agent does a whole lot more than open the door to a listing (though we love to do that as well). Your Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement will spell out these services, including: locating and presenting properties, writing offers, consulting with you on the loan process, providing vendor recommendations for inspections, negotiating on your behalf, guiding you through escrow, and more.

### Are Buyer-Broker Representation Agreements Exclusive?

Most agreements are exclusive, meaning you cannot work with another buyer's agent during the term. So, if you've been playing the field with other agents, it's time to make a choice. There is a significantly different level of hustle when your agent is your one and only.

### How Does a Buyer's Agent Get Paid?

Buyer's agents' commissions can vary, and this agreement provides transparency on your agent's commission from the jump. What is your agent's commission, and what happens if the seller does not cover it? This section deserves your full attention. Read it carefully and ask questions before you sign.

### Can I Terminate a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement Early?

Presumably, you're not planning to end your relationship with your real estate agent when you sign this agreement. That said: stuff happens. The termination clause will spell out what the process looks like if the relationship does not work out, including your options for exiting the agreement early. A good agent will be willing to discuss this honestly.

## What Is Standard Buyer's Agent Commission in California?

This is the question everyone is asking right now, and the honest answer is: it depends, and it has changed significantly.

For decades, seller's agents and buyer's agents split a commission that was baked into the sale price, typically somewhere around 5 to 6 percent total, split between both sides. Buyers never saw this negotiated directly. It just happened.

That changed following a landmark legal settlement in 2024. The way buyer agent compensation works is now fundamentally different:

- Your agent's fee is negotiated directly between you and your agent, not set by the seller
- In many transactions, you can ask the seller to cover some or all of your agent's fee as a concession. The seller may accept, counter, or decline.
- If the seller declines to cover the fee, you as the buyer are responsible for the agreed amount

There is no single standard commission anymore. Rates vary and are negotiable. What matters is that you understand exactly what you have agreed to pay before you tour a single home.

Your Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement will spell this out. Read that section carefully, ask your agent to walk you through it, and make sure you are comfortable with the terms before signing.

## What to Ask Before You Sign a Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement

A good agent will welcome these questions. If yours does not, that is useful information.

- What services are included in your representation?
- How long is this agreement and what does the exit process look like?
- How is your compensation structured?
- What happens if the seller does not offer to cover your fee?
- Are there any additional fees I should know about upfront?
- Will you walk me through this agreement line by line before I sign?

You are not being difficult by asking these questions. You are being a prepared buyer.

## What Happens If I Do Not Sign?

In California, your agent legally cannot tour homes with you without a signed agreement. This is not a preference or a brokerage policy. And for those of us who are stoked to just start house hunting with you already, it can be a bit of a bummer.

But listen: The agreement is not a trap. It is a protection. It means everyone knows exactly what the relationship is before you spend a single Saturday afternoon at an open house.

## One Last Thing

A Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement does not mean you are locked into buying a home, committed to a specific price range, or obligated to make an offer on anything. It means you and your agent have agreed on how you will work together. That is all.
