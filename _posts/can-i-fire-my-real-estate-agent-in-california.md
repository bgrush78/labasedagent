I get this question more than you'd think — usually from a buyer or seller who's already frustrated and just wants out. The short answer is yes, you can fire your agent in California. The longer answer is that how you do it, and whether it costs you anything, depends entirely on what you signed and when.

Since January 2025, that "what you signed" question matters more than it used to. Here's the breakdown.

## The Rule That Changed Everything: AB 2992

**As of January 1, 2025, California requires buyers to sign a written buyer-broker agreement before they can tour a property with an agent.** That's a direct result of the national NAR settlement, and it's enforced by the DRE. A few years ago, buyers could wander through open houses and showings for weeks before signing anything binding. That grace period is gone.

Practically, this means almost every buyer working with an agent today is already under contract earlier in the relationship than they used to be. If things go sideways with your agent, you're not walking away from a handshake deal — you're walking away from paperwork, and that paperwork has terms.

There's one important escape hatch built into AB 2992: the single-property agreement. If your agent had you sign a separate form for each individual showing, you're not locked in beyond that one property. Each agreement dies at the end of that showing. If you shopped around this way before settling on someone, your exposure is minimal.

If instead you signed one exclusive buyer-broker agreement covering a date range or a list of homes, that's a different conversation — and the one most people calling me about are actually in.

Worth noting too: the same NAR settlement pulled seller-paid buyer commission offers out of the MLS entirely. Buyer agent compensation now gets negotiated directly between you and your agent when you sign. That number, whatever it is, is part of what determines what you owe (if anything) if you terminate early.

## Buyers vs. Sellers: Different Contracts, Different Exits

Which side of the deal you're on changes everything about how termination works.

Buyers sign a buyer-broker (or buyer representation) agreement. A single-property version expires after one showing with no strings attached. An exclusive version usually requires written notice to end, and may still cover you for any home the agent physically introduced you to, even after you cancel.

Sellers sign a listing agreement — most commonly the CAR Residential Listing Agreement (RLA). This one's stickier. Ending it typically requires the agent's written consent, or a documented reason (agents don't have to just let you go). And even after you're released, most listing agreements carry a safety clause — sometimes called a protection period — that runs 30 to 90 days. If a buyer the agent introduced during the listing later closes on the home, commission can still be owed, even though the agreement is technically over.

Sellers also have lower-commitment options: an exclusive agency listing lets you sell the home yourself (FSBO) without owing commission, and an open listing only pays whoever actually brings the buyer.

## The Safety Clause: The Part That Actually Costs People Money

This is where most disputes come from, and it's worth sitting with for a second.

The safety clause exists to protect the agent's work. If they spent weeks marketing your home, hosting showings, and negotiating with a buyer, and you fire them the week before that buyer makes an offer, the clause is what keeps them from losing the commission entirely. Fair in principle. Painful in practice if you didn't know it was there.

The number that matters is how many days the clause runs (check your RLA — 30 to 90 days is standard) and whether the agent can actually document that the buyer was introduced during the listing term. If both of those line up, you may owe commission even to an agent you've already fired.

## Procuring Cause: Why It Matters If You're Switching Agents Mid-Search

If you're a buyer switching agents rather than walking away from the process entirely, ask about procuring cause — essentially, which agent actually did the work that led to the eventual sale. If your old agent showed you a house and your new agent writes the offer on that same house, you can end up in a dispute between the two agents (or brokerages) over who's owed. This gets sorted out through MLS arbitration more often than it gets sorted out by you, but it's smart to know it exists before you sign anything new.

## Is This Worth Doing, or Is It Just Normal Friction?

Not every frustration is a fireable offense. Slow response times during a busy week, a showing that got rescheduled, a negotiating strategy you didn't love in the moment — that's often just the process being the process.

I'd actually pick up the phone and have the termination conversation when:

- Your agent isn't disclosing offers, communications, or material facts about the property
- You've asked for basic updates and gotten silence for a week or more
- There's a real conflict of interest (dual agency you weren't fully briefed on, a side relationship with the other party)
- You've simply lost trust in their advice on price, timing, or negotiation

If it's one of those, don't just go dark on your agent. Put it in writing.

## Sending Written Notice

However you're terminating, keep it simple and in writing — email is fine, but many termination clauses ask for it explicitly. Reference the agreement by date, state clearly that you're terminating effective immediately (or per the notice period in your contract), and send it to both the agent and their brokerage, not just the agent personally. Keep a copy for your own records — if a safety clause dispute comes up later, this is the document that shows when the clock started.

## If It Escalates: The DRE and Small Claims

If your agent won't release you, or you believe they've violated their fiduciary duty, you can file a complaint with the California Department of Real Estate. For commission disputes specifically, California's small claims court handles claims up to $12,500, which covers a lot of these situations without needing an attorney.

## Bottom Line

Read the agreement before you send the text. Know whether you're dealing with a single-property form or an exclusive agreement. If you're a seller, find the safety clause and know your numbers. And if you're not sure what you signed, that's a five-minute phone call, not a five-hour research project.
