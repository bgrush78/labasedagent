**The right questions to ask a real estate agent cover communication style, experience, local market knowledge, and how they're compensated, whether you're buying or selling.**

Recently, I was in the market for sleep headphones (I know: I live a wild and glamorous life). I read just about every guide out there, a surprisingly high number to be honest, and went down some deeply impassioned Reddit threads. This process took months, during which I hunted for my AirPods in my sheets every single morning. And, again: this was for sleep headphones, a hundred-ish dollar purchase.

Yet the first time I purchased a house in Los Angeles, which ended up costing just a touch more than a hundred-ish dollars, my research into real estate agents consisted of asking two friends for their agent's contact info.

And my first time home buying self was not alone in this light touch research. According to the [National Association of Realtors](https://www.nar.realtor/)' 2025 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, most buyers interviewed only one agent before deciding who to work with, true for 67% of first-time buyers and 76% of repeat buyers, with another 16% interviewing two agents.

So… yes. People are putting more research into sleep headphones than into choosing the person who's going to help them with the biggest financial transaction of their life.

I get it. Agents are everywhere. Every open house has one, every bus bench has a face on it, and every Google search has a sponsored ad with one. But here's the thing: just like you wouldn't hire the first contractor who shows up with a business card, you shouldn't pick an agent without a real conversation first.

If you're a first time buyer just starting your [home search](https://www.lametrohomefinder.com/team/brian-grushkin), this can feel especially intimidating. You don't know what you don't know, and the agent you choose is going to be your guide through almost everything: paperwork, choosing a lender, negotiations, inspections, and a hundred small decisions that add up fast. If you're selling, the stakes are different but just as high; this is your equity, your timeline, your "for sale" sign on the lawn.

So whether you're buying your first home, your fifth, or getting ready to sell, here's what I'd ask (and did ask the second time around) before signing anything.

## If You're Buying: Questions to Ask a Real Estate Agent

### How will you communicate with me, and how fast can I expect to hear back?

This one sounds basic, but it might be the most important question on this list. Buying a home moves fast, especially when you find "the one" on a Friday night and three other people found it too. If your agent takes two days to respond to a text, that house could be gone before you even get a showing.

Ask how they prefer to communicate (text, email, calls, carrier pigeon) and what kind of response time is realistic. My rule for myself: responsive, not robotic. You should hear back the same day, usually within the hour.

### What does the Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement actually cover, and will you walk me through it?

As of 2025, every buyer's agent in California has to sign a [Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement](/blog/buyer-broker-representation-agreement-california) with you before touring homes. It's not a trick, and it's not specific to one brokerage; it's the law. But you should absolutely understand what you're signing before you sign it.

Ask your agent to explain how long the agreement lasts, how their compensation works, and what happens if the seller doesn't cover the fee. A good agent will go through this line by line with you, no rush, no pressure. If they brush past it or seem annoyed you asked, that's useful information too.

### What's your actual experience, and what does that look like in practice?

Years in the business sound impressive on a business card, but they don't tell you much about how someone actually operates. What matters more is how they negotiate, how they prep you for what's coming, and how they handle it when something goes sideways (because something usually does).

If an agent is newer to the business, ask what they're bringing with them. Sometimes a fresh perspective paired with serious experience in another field, marketing, project management, customer service, can be just as valuable as a decade of repetition. Don't be afraid to ask directly: "How long have you been licensed, and what were you doing before this?"

### How do you help buyers compete in this market?

Multiple offer situations are still very real in Los Angeles. Ask what your agent's strategy is for getting your offer noticed: how they write it, how they communicate with listing agents, and whether they have relationships with lenders and inspectors that can help things move quickly when it counts.

### Do you actually know this area?

If you're looking in Los Angeles, the San Gabriel Valley, or anywhere in Southern California, local knowledge isn't a nice-to-have. Ask what your agent knows about the schools, the commute, the fire zones, the HOA quirks, and all the little things that never make it onto a listing but absolutely affect your life once you're living there.

### What happens after we close?

A good agent doesn't vanish the second you get your keys. Ask what kind of support continues after closing. Do they have go-to people for contractors, landscapers, or random "who do I call about this" situations? Could you text them six months from now with a random question? (The answer should be yes.)

## If You're Selling: Questions to Ask a Real Estate Agent

### How will you market my home, specifically?

This is the big one, and it's where a lot of agents get vague. "I'll list it on the MLS and put it on social media" is not a marketing plan, that's the bare minimum.

Ask to see examples of their photography, their listing descriptions, and how they actually promote a home once it's live. Do they create video content? Do they run any kind of paid promotion? How do they make sure your home doesn't just blend in with the fifteen other listings someone is scrolling past on their phone? If marketing is your agent's background (hi!), this is exactly where it shows.

### How did you land on this price?

Ask your agent to actually walk you through their pricing strategy: what comparable homes they're looking at, what's happening in the market right now, and why they landed where they did. A good agent explains their reasoning. A number pulled out of thin air is not a strategy.

### What will communication look like during the listing process?

Selling a home involves a lot of moving pieces: showings, feedback, offers, negotiations, more feedback. Ask how often you'll hear from your agent and in what format. You should never be left wondering what's going on with your own listing.

### Will you help me prepare the home before it goes live?

Ask whether your agent offers guidance on staging, repairs, or small updates that could make a real difference in how buyers see your home. Do they have vendors they trust if you need help getting things ready?

### How do you handle multiple offers?

If your home gets more than one offer (and in this market, it might), ask how your agent helps you evaluate them. Price matters, but so do contingencies, financing, and timelines. You want an agent who can break all of that down for you in plain English, not just hand you a stack of offers and a shrug.

### What's the plan if it doesn't sell right away?

Ask how your agent would adjust if the home isn't getting the activity you expected. A good agent has a plan B before you even need one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many real estate agents should I interview before choosing one?

There's no required number, but most buyers only talk to one. According to NAR's 2025 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, 67% of first-time buyers and 76% of repeat buyers interviewed only a single agent. Talking to even one or two more can give you a real basis for comparison on communication style, experience, and local knowledge before you commit.

### Is it free to talk to a real estate agent before signing anything?

Yes. An initial conversation to ask questions and see if you're a good fit doesn't cost anything and doesn't obligate you to work together. In California, you'll only need to sign a [Buyer-Broker Representation Agreement](/blog/buyer-broker-representation-agreement-california) once you're ready to actually tour homes with that agent.

### What's the most important question to ask a real estate agent?

There isn't one single most important question, but communication style is a good place to start. How an agent answers "how and when will you communicate with me" often tells you a lot about what working with them will actually feel like.

### Should buyers and sellers ask different questions?

Some questions overlap, like communication style and experience, but buyers should focus more on local market knowledge and how an agent helps them compete for a home, while sellers should focus more on marketing strategy and pricing approach.

## The Bottom Line

The right agent is someone who communicates the way you actually need them to, knows the area you're buying or selling in, and brings something real to the table, whether that's years of deals or a different kind of professional background that translates well.

Don't be afraid to interview more than one agent. Don't be afraid to ask the awkward questions. Any agent worth hiring will welcome them, and honestly, it'll tell you a lot about what it's like to work with them before you even sign anything.
